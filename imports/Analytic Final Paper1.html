Clear As Mud
Throughout the semester, Ive been trying to understand not only what McDowell and
Zwicky are doing respectively, but also what their work means together. Is there a together? Or
are they inherently opposed? I dont think that theyre opposed. Instead, I think that they are
aligned; but I dont think that theyre saying the same thing. I believe that McDowell and Zwicky
are generally in agreement about their end goal of understanding the world through embracing
ambiguity. However, they are tackling issues in different areas. Its the difference in where
theyre attacking that I think makes it seem as though they are more different than they really
are. I plan to show what McDowell and Zwicky are doing and how they line up. I believe that
this view is further evidence of philosophy as understanding or clarifying, but where does it
leave us? Is clarity enough?
McDowells project can be generally seen as philosophical therapy for the problems
weve indoctrinated ourselves with. McDowell is all about diagnosing that problem. He is
pointing at notions trying to make you see what he means. McDowell offers his solution, but
ultimately Mind and World is about making the reader realize the problem for themselves.
McDowell cannot just tell you that the gap between mind and world is perceived and that in
reality receptivity and spontaneity are not notionally separable. Even if you understand what
those words mean, you wont see it as McDowell does.
MacAulay 2
We have our own way of seeing the world, whether philosophically backed or not. What
makes this even harder is the intolerable oscillation that McDowell describes. The extremes of
Givenism vs Coherentism pull you either way. Even if not apparent to you, the world enforces
views that fall on the spectrum between the two extremes. You can try to balance as much as you
want, but institutions, genetics, culture, conceptual inferences, developmental factors, and
biological mechanisms whisper in your ear, and suddenly youre oscillating again. McDowell
finds this intolerable, for it doesnt accurately represent the world and it is actively unpleasant to
live with. The solution McDowell gives is to reject the dualism; to stop further risk of oscillation.
There is a danger of falling into an interminable oscillation. But we can find a way to dismount
from the seesaw. (McDowell 9). But what does it mean to step away from the dualism, opposed
to taking the centre position? The two can look similar. The difference is that you are not stuck in
the middle; you are taking a new position of understanding the world. This is different because it
offers a holistic view, rather than a piecewise one. This is all assuming that you as the reader are
doing the work yourself.
Brandom and Rorty agreed with McDowell on his dealing with the dualism but said it
was irresponsible to suggest this to others because McDowell is a skilled professional whereas
others might not be. I believe that they missed the point of Mind and World here, because that is
what its doing. The book is about making the reader realize this for themselves, not about telling
the right view. McDowell says just this: We cannot satisfactorily splice spontaneity and
receptivity together in our conception of experience, and that means we cannot exploit the
Kantian thought that the realm of law, not just the realm of meaningful doings, is not external to
the conceptual. The understanding-the very capacity that we bring to bear on texts-must be
involved in our taking in of mere meaningless happenings. (McDowell 97). McDowell does

Zwickys project begins by introducing Gestalt. What is it? "The word Gestalt means
shape or form (Zwicky 4). Why is it important? As technocracy has burgeoned, Gestalt
comprehension has become less and less visible as a way of understanding the world. (Zwicky
28). Further questions and capabilities of Gestalt are answered throughout the book, but its
important that the reader understands what Zwicky sees in it. many of us live now caught up
in the nexus of Big Data. We find this situation troubling but feel powerless to escape. How did
Big Data come to dominate our lives? It did so in part through an alliance between capitalism
and mechanistic technology (Zwicky VIII). By being open to the existence of Gestalt, the
reader can begin to see the imbalance everywhere around them. Like McDowell pointing at
notions, Zwicky does the same. Instead of just relying on telling the reader how the world is,
Zwicky uses many different sources such as math, poetry, art, Gestalt images, etc., to repeatedly
push the point in the readers face until they have a Gestalt shift. As mentioned, the reader is
furthering their learning about Gestalt through the point Zwicky is making and through the ways
she presents the point. Allowing the reader to themselves see how the Gestaltic image is formed
and is everywhere in the world.
MacAulay 4
Zwicky makes the reader see the technocracy around them, actively stopping them from
achieving resonance. Our puzzlement is cultural. It is a product of making explicit, analyzable
structure the paradigm of meaning. We thereby sculpt intelligence according to a technocratic
ideal. (Zwicky 134). Zwicky sees it as an imbalance and the way to fix it is through balancing.
The experience of meaning is the experience of resonant relationship. The deeper and more
complex the resonance, the more powerful the experience of meaning. When a mode of
representation curtails resonance, it curtails the experience of meaning. (Zwicky 135). Balance
is gained from understanding and embracing the Gestalt and calculative world holistically. This
may set off alarm bells, as McDowell suggests you can never balance. The difference is that
Zwickys balancing of the hemispheres is not the same issue as balancing mind and world. The
hemispheres are fundamental aspects of our lives as humans. They are not contrived extremes,
rather they are like the wheels on a bicycle. If one wheel is not being maintained and all the work
is being put onto the other one, there are going to be problems. You might say that unicycles
work fine, but were not unicycles; were bicycles which are made to utilize two wheels to
function. McDowells dualism is something that we created, and its enforced by the world.
Zwicky is dealing with our imbalance of life, which has created enforcement in the world. The
technocracy was born out of the imbalance, whereas McDowells dualism was created by it.
One of the big differences between McDowell and Zwicky is their views on language.
For McDowell, the problems in philosophy are linguistic, but language is not a barrier. Its how
we view language that is the barrier. McDowell believes that we can get past this, not by raising
language up or bringing it down, but by understanding our goals with it. Zwickys views on
language are that it is useful for communicating, but overreliance on it for clarity is bound to lead
to problems. The technocracy sees language as a clear way to communicate where Zwicky would

say it could never communicate clearly as experience does. You can utilize language to try to
express experience through poetry, lyric philosophy, or metaphors. However, all those fall short
of actual experience and are using language in tandem with other things to be more resonant with
the world. So, perhaps McDowell and Zwicky arent as opposed on language as originally
thought. While McDowell places more value on language as the main problem, him and Zwicky
arent opposing each other on what language is and can do. They have a similar understanding

While McDowell and Zwicky are tackling different issues, ultimately, they have similar
or synonymous end goals. Both want us to realize for ourselves that there is something wrong.
McDowell shows how the dualism is plaguing our thoughts, and that the rejection of ambiguity
is what enforces extremism and oscillation. Zwicky shows how there is a fundamental imbalance
coming from the rejections of ambiguity enforcing a structure that keeps up in a state of lacking
resonance. Both McDowell and Zwicky want us to embrace and deal with ambiguity to see the
greater picture. Once youve begun to see the ambiguity of the world, you can work on living
your life through that ambiguity, virtuously. This cultivates a life that doesnt deal in parts and
instead utilizes all the world has to offer.
Despite similarity in goal, McDowell and Zwicky are dealing with separate issues within
this project. The dualism McDowell is concerned with between Givenism and Coherentism, is
only something that occurs within the technocracy. Only within a structure that is missing a
fundamental pillar but trying to make do anyways could create a dualism like this. It is the lack
of resonance creating the oscillating nature, the lack of tension that causes the notes on the lyre
to not ring out true. The note that should be a C sharp sounds more like a C flat, and sometimes
like a regular C in the technocracy. Youre not sure what note was played because you keep

The imbalance of Gestalt and calculative has led to the creation of the technocracy but it
is the continued existence of the technocracy which keeps that imbalance from ever balancing.
We habitually experience certain emotions in certain situations, they often become aspects of
our gestalt of those situations. This creates an epistemologically complex situation in which truth
can be obscured. Or illuminated. (Zwicky 124). McDowell is seeing that dualisms like mind
and world arise and are enforced by the world we live in. Through McDowells second nature, or
Zwickys spiritual insight, hopefully we can cultivate a virtuous life by embracing ambiguity. By
respecting the world, we can live a just and meaningful life. Despite the craziness and problems
we face, the world still exists, whether we see it or not. Both McDowell and Zwicky advocate for
a type of producing as humans. By production I mean being in the world in an active way.
Producing as a human is living your life, not being confined to the manufactured limitations. A
way of understanding how you can produce as a human is through engaging in spiritual insight
or second nature.
Zwickys spiritual insight is a way of accepting the ambiguity of the world by embracing
spirituality. In many cultures, its taken for granted that there are important connections among
poetry, philosophy, and what might be called spiritual insight. (Zwicky 147). This means
acknowledging that the world has potential to offer more than you realize. An example could be
that I dont believe in ghosts. Ive had enough interactions with the world in which I havent had
evidence to believe in them. Yet, despite my disbelief, I dont deny their potential existence.
What good would it do me to deny. I dont know all, I could never say with full confidence that

Its through acts of resonance with our Gestalt and calculative aspects that the world
appears to be clear. I personally find this with comedy. It fascinates me endlessly. Whats funny?
Why is that funny? The subtleties of the execution. You can dissect and analytically determine
whats funny and why. You can run a bit endlessly to perfect the execution. These things are
crucial to honing the craft but so is intuitively letting jokes flow through you. Sometimes
amazing jokes arent crafted at all; they happen spontaneously, whereas a joke perfectly crafted
can fall flat. Having mastery of both allows for the spontaneity of the knowledge of comedy to
be not notionally separable from the receptivity of comedy on the fly. Not to say the two ways
are entirely equal, but they are so intertwined that they cannot be separate. The same goes for
poetry, music, writing, math, etc. To play of the musical phrase in, against, over and through
the sentence is what creates the amazingly complex texture of good poetry, whether free or
formal. (Zwicky 47). Everything has a cap when you try to separate the two. But when the two
are not notionally separable, you can now throw away the ladder that once served as a crutch,
and true production can be born.

Whats been interesting to me is that people who Im almost certain havent read either
McDowell or Zwicky (specifically Zwicky), say things that are almost like quotes from the
books. The type of people Im talking about understand the experience of meaning despite
potentially not having the developed explanation and conclusion that Zwicky does. Im talking
about artists. People who truly understand art seem to be able to also understand the ambiguity of
the world. Perhaps its because to make true art, you need to use wholes, not parts. Or perhaps
its because they were ostracized from society and were not incentivised to conform by it. Or
perhaps they are experienced in expressing their experiences to the world. Or perhaps they are
better at empathizing. Its likely all those reasons and more. I think its often assumed that artists
are only trained in art and reject the rest of the world, but to make good art I think thatd be
impossible. To make good art, you need to be involved in the ambiguity of the world and be
connected to it.
While Ive seen more artists seem to implicitly understand the experience of meaning on
their own, I dont think you have to be an artist to do this. I think that the technocracy has just
created a space where people in STEM, business, and similar fields are rewarded for rejecting
Gestalt and the ambiguity of the world. Theres nothing inherent about artists that cause them to
be able to accept ambiguity. Its exactly because artists can see the experience of meaning, where
others cant, that shows how the technocracy shifts and changes the world. Ive heard stories of
the past that tell a different story of scientists. Inventors who explored the world and wanted to
understand its every fiber, yet they didnt presume to know all, and they used various methods of
discovery. This reminds me of the lifestyle of wizards and witches. The whimsical, wise lifestyle
has caught the eyes of many who are fed up with the constraints of modern society. People who
